

# **The English Sweating Sickness: A Mechanistic, Interdisciplinary Hypothesis**

## **Abstract**
This repository presents a mechanistic, historically grounded hypothesis for the cause of the English Sweating Sickness (1485–1551). The model integrates period medical terminology, survivor symptom patterns, fatal-phase progression, toxicological plausibility, supply‑chain context, and linguistic analysis. The goal is to provide a falsifiable, academically structured framework suitable for evaluation by historians, toxicologists, epidemiologists, and archaeoscientists.

Two core documents form the basis of the hypothesis:

- **EnglishSweate_Hypo.pdf** — Primary mechanistic hypothesis  
- **WSweate_SecondaryPath.pdf** — Secondary pathway and supporting analysis

## **1. Historical Context**
The English Sweating Sickness was a rapidly fatal epidemic syndrome characterized by sudden onset, extreme prostration, profuse sweating, and mortality within hours. Contemporary descriptions are linguistically dense, medically pre‑Galenic in framing, and often misinterpreted through modern clinical categories. This repository prioritizes period‑accurate terminology and avoids anachronistic flattening.

---

## **2. Core Hypothesis (Summary)**
The working model proposes that the English Sweating Sickness represents a **toxicological syndrome** rather than a classical infectious disease. The hypothesis is built from:

- Survivor symptom patterns  
- Fatal-phase progression  
- Distinctive autonomic and neuromuscular features  
- Geographic and temporal clustering  
- Supply-chain and environmental vectors  
- Period medical language (flux, sweat, stinking sweat, inward heat, oppression, etc.)  
- Comparative analysis with known toxicological agents  

The full mechanistic argument is detailed in the PDFs.

## **3. Symptom Progression Overview**

### **Survivor Pattern**
- Sudden onset of dread or foreboding  
- Heat, oppression, or inward burning  
- Profuse sweating  
- Thirst, restlessness, or agitation  
- Resolution after prolonged sweating  

### **Fatal Pattern**
- Abrupt collapse  
- Cardiorespiratory distress  
- Cold or “stinking” sweat  
- Delirium or confusion  
- Death within 3–18 hours  

A full structured table is included in the hypothesis documents.

## **4. Methodology**
This hypothesis is constructed using interdisciplinary methods:

- **Historical linguistics** — period‑specific medical vocabulary, semantic drift, and Galenic framing  
- **Primary source analysis** — symptom extraction and cross‑text comparison  
- **Toxicology** — mechanistic modeling of dose, exposure, and physiological response  
- **Environmental and supply‑chain history** — trade routes, storage conditions, contamination vectors  
- **Comparative pathology** — differential analysis against plague, influenza, anthrax, ergotism, and other candidates  

The model is explicitly **falsifiable** and designed for archaeotoxicological testing.

---

## **5. Archaeotoxicology Sampling Framework**
To evaluate the hypothesis, the following components are proposed:

- **Target sites** — burial grounds associated with documented Sweating Sickness outbreaks  
- **Biomarkers** — expected metabolites and degradation products  
- **Controls** — negative (non‑outbreak burials) and positive (known toxic exposures)  
- **Contamination considerations** — soil chemistry, groundwater, and post‑burial alteration  
- **Analytical methods** — LC‑MS/MS, GC‑MS, and targeted metabolite screening  

A detailed sampling strategy is included in the secondary pathway document.

## **6. Glossary of Period Medical Terms**
A full glossary is maintained in the hypothesis documents. Key terms include:

- **Sweate / Sweat** — not equivalent to modern perspiration; often implies pathological flux  
- **Stinking Sweat** — a diagnostic marker with toxicological implications  
- **Oppression** — pressure, constriction, or thoracic distress  
- **Inward Heat** — autonomic dysregulation, not fever  
- **Flux** — uncontrolled bodily discharge or systemic purgation  

These terms are interpreted within their 15th–16th century semantic range.

## **7. Evaluation Criteria**
Researchers can evaluate the hypothesis using:

- Symptom concordance  
- Mechanistic plausibility  
- Geographic and temporal fit  
- Toxicological dose‑response alignment  
- Compatibility with primary sources  
- Archaeological biomarker detectability  

The hypothesis is intended as a structured, testable model rather than a definitive claim.
